# Design principles

Use these as design constraints, not as a lecture to the child.

## Reading ownership

- Let the child choose order, activity, output medium, stopping point, and whether to share.
- Preserve the right to abandon, pause, skim, reread, listen, or switch editions.
- Do not make every act of reading produce a visible artifact.

## Useful learning moves

- **Prediction:** Form a reasoned expectation from title, illustration, structure, or prior events; revisit it later.
- **Retrieval:** Close the book before recalling a scene, causal chain, idea, image, or question.
- **Spacing:** Return to selected ideas after a day or week rather than testing everything immediately.
- **Elaboration:** Connect new material to another text, experience, place, observation, or question.
- **Dual representation:** Translate selectively between words and maps, diagrams, gestures, objects, audio, or images.
- **Chunking:** Divide long works by meaningful episodes, problems, journeys, or arguments rather than arbitrary page quotas.
- **Metacognition:** Help the child notice which strategy helped: rereading, slowing down, using context, listening, mapping, or asking for help.
- **Desirable challenge:** Offer a reachable stretch with a bridge; avoid both effortless busywork and chronic confusion.

## Evidence without schoolification

Good traces capture a decision, discovery, question, connection, textual clue, strategy, or change of mind. Weak traces merely prove compliance, count pages, copy sentences, or reproduce a fixed answer.

## Emotional and cultural care

- Allow dislike, discomfort, disagreement, and uncertainty.
- For grief, violence, discrimination, colonial contexts, or frightening material, provide a content note and an optional pause/bridge path.
- Do not require personal disclosure to demonstrate understanding.
- When comparing cultures or historical periods, distinguish the text's perspective from present-day endorsement.

## Difficulty adjustment

If too hard, offer one bridge at a time: preview a summary, listen while following text, narrow the excerpt, supply a character map, clarify five essential words, or switch to shared/assisted reading temporarily.

If too easy, deepen the reading move instead of adding volume: compare accounts, detect narrative choices, test a claim, redesign a solution, or trace a motif.

If interest falls, allow a route change: switch format, select a different chapter, alternate with a shorter book, or pause without penalty.
