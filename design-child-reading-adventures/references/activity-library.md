# Activity library

Select a small, varied set. Adapt wording and difficulty to the child. Never use activities as substitutes for reading.

## Text-dependence gate

Every core activity must receive something from a protected reading chunk and use it to change the play. Name both parts:

1. **text input**: a detail, obstacle, decision, relationship, rule, or causal pattern the child notices or chooses from the inspected text;
2. **play effect**: the route, rule, available choice, or consequence that changes because of that input.

Run the book-removal test. If the same activity could be used with an unrelated story by changing only the title, character labels, or decoration, it is a generic game wearing a story skin; reject or rebuild it. Keep the reading move non-graded: the child may choose what to carry into play, and no adult quiz or single expected answer is required.

When content is verified, only use inputs supported by that evidence. When exact content is unavailable, invite the child to select something from the section just read and make it alter the play; do not supply invented specifics.

## Open-play recipe

Use these patterns before reaching for worksheets or fixed response formats:

- **world interruption**: something in the story world breaks, multiplies, disappears, changes size, or gains a new rule; the child decides what happens next;
- **loose-parts build**: use safe nearby objects, paper, blocks, string, or scrap to build a place, path, machine, barrier, creature, or repair;
- **floor or tabletop world**: turn a small area into the story space, then move one element and follow the consequences;
- **power with a price**: let the child invent a power, decide its limit and cost, and test it in a story moment;
- **solo role-play**: use voices, gesture, toys, shadows, or recording; the child may switch roles or add a new witness;
- **sound and movement system**: create a weather machine, danger signal, travel rhythm, creature call, or ritual from ordinary objects;
- **impossible job lab**: attempt a huge task with ten tiny moves, then freely continue, redesign, recruit, or abandon;
- **branching remix**: change one decision or rule and play forward until the world reaches a new problem;
- **found-object transformation**: let a nearby object become a mythic tool, creature, landscape, message, or portal;
- **child-made rule**: invent a world law, then test it against two moments from the story.

For each pattern, carry one true detail from the text into play and stop while the child still wants more. The trace may be a moved object, a temporary arrangement, a voice memo, a photo, a name, a symbol, or nothing.

## Toy-like mechanics for younger independent readers

Use these as mechanics, not as rewards for correct answers. Give each one a real story detail to carry and enough freedom for the child to change the rules.

- **clay portrait or creature**: model a character, tool, monster, landscape, or power; require only one text-grounded feature, then let the child choose scale, materials, pose, extra powers, and ending;
- **tabletop target game**: turn paper balls, cups, cards, or safe soft objects into suns, gates, mountains, boats, or clues; let the child invent scoring or deliberately remove scoring;
- **stamp or footprint trail**: stamp after reading, rereading, playing, wondering, or telling—not only after finishing; the mark can be invented each time and never becomes a streak requirement;
- **story-dependent navigation**: begin only after a reading move; turn verified obstacles, decisions, relationships, or causes into route choices, rules, or consequences. A story-named start and finish around generic walls is not enough. At least one route must change because of what the child noticed in the text, and the child may add, remove, or rewrite a wall, shortcut, creature, destination, consequence, or rule;
- **connect-your-own-path**: scatter story objects, sounds, characters, or places and let the child decide which ones to connect and what the line means; do not hide one correct relationship answer;
- **story courier**: introduce the story to another child through a 30–90 second voice message, puppet, object show, teaser, or recommendation; solo mode addresses a toy, imaginary listener, or recorder;
- **sound track**: connect or arrange sound icons, then perform them with voice, hands, or safe objects; the child controls rhythm, volume, order, and ending;
- **living mind map**: begin with an image such as antlers, roots, clouds, waves, pockets, or roads; branches may hold drawings, words, objects, questions, or invented offshoots, and blank branches are allowed;
- **photo proof without worksheet proof**: a photo of a temporary model, route, performance setup, or clay figure is a complete record; offer a tiny caption only if the child wants one.

Across one handbook, avoid repeating the same mechanic more than twice. Do not follow a physical game with a compulsory written explanation of what it taught.

## Child-perceived play-rhythm audit

Before designing a handbook with four or more core activities, make a parent-only sequence with one **primary interaction grammar** for each activity. A grammar is the child’s central decision loop—not the book detail, title, prop, illustration, output medium, or optional trace.

Useful distinct grammars include allocating scarce resources, inferring from clues, choosing a route with consequences, building then testing, interviewing or answering in role, revising a rule, encoding or decoding with sound/signals, comparing competing evidence, simulating a system, and transforming one constraint into another. Combine or invent grammars when the text calls for it; do not treat this list as a required menu.

- With four or more core activities, do not repeat a primary grammar in adjacent activities, use one grammar at most twice, and use at least four grammars overall.
- With eight or more core activities, use at least six grammars overall.
- Treat activities as the same grammar when only their story anchor, title, props, page layout, trace, or final artifact changes. For example, “notice an obstacle then draw a route,” “notice a decision then build a route,” and “notice a relationship then arrange a route” remain one route-arranging loop unless the child’s actual decision process changes.
- A child’s stated love of one medium can justify a repeated medium, but not a false claim of varied play. Keep the medium and vary the decision loop where possible; explain any exception in the adult audit.

Keep this audit adult-facing. On child pages, make the change visible through genuinely different invitations and play surfaces, never through labels or compliance counts.

## Agency test

Reject or reopen an activity when:

- every successful result would look nearly the same;
- the child mainly fills blanks, reports, labels, summarizes, or demonstrates comprehension;
- all materials, steps, roles, sequence, and output are predetermined;
- a child may choose only colors, names, decoration, or how to word the same expected answer;
- goal, rules, and successful end state are all fixed, even if the final step says “now change anything”;
- the playful title can be removed without changing a worksheet-like task;
- the activity needs adult praise, questioning, judgment, or performance management to feel complete.

Keep at least three meaningful dimensions open: goal, material, role, method, order, rule, scale, voice, ending, partner, output, or whether to leave a trace. A dimension counts only when another choice would materially change the process or outcome. At least one of goal, rule, or ending must be changeable, abandonable, or allowed to remain unresolved.

Only the text anchor and necessary safety／stop boundary are mandatory. Offer the rest as replaceable starting possibilities. Say visibly that the child may remix, reorder, switch medium, refuse the extension, stop, or leave no trace. Do not prescribe goal, materials, method, order, rules, and end state together.

## Entry scaffold pattern

For an open play that is not immediately obvious, use this compact pattern:

1. **Imagine the first frame**: tell the child what has just happened without assigning a required goal.
2. **Gather or choose**: suggest 2–4 common material types, plus a no-material option; every suggestion is replaceable.
3. **Offer first moves**: provide two or more possible observable actions such as place, move, divide, connect, give a voice, or change a rule; they may be reordered or ignored.
4. **Offer a disturbance**: invite the child to change one element and follow what happens, or create a different disturbance.
5. **Hand over control**: explicitly release at least one of the goal, rule, or ending, plus two other meaningful dimensions.

Example: `Something you just read has entered this tabletop world. You might place it, give it a voice, move another object, or change a rule—or start another way. Decide what this world wants, what can change, and whether it ever reaches an ending. Stop or leave nothing behind whenever you like.` This demonstrates play grammar without silently fixing the goal, materials, sequence, rule, or result.

## Cross-genre activity families

### Notice and collect

- clue catcher: collect three details that alter a prediction;
- word museum: keep one vivid, puzzling, funny, or powerful expression and explain the choice;
- pattern tracker: mark recurrence of an image, object, question, or behavior;
- question parking lot: save questions without requiring immediate answers.

### Decide and test

- prediction ticket: choose among plausible outcomes and cite one clue;
- decision fork: make a character's or researcher's next decision, then compare with the text;
- claim lab: mark “supported / uncertain / challenged” and collect evidence;
- constraint challenge: solve a textual problem with limited resources.

### Map and model

- journey map, relationship web, process diagram, scale model, timeline, system map;
- before/after map showing what changed and why;
- invisible map for emotion, power, danger, sound, or uncertainty.

### Speak and perform

- one-minute field report;
- audio postcard from a setting or historical moment;
- object interview or character hot seat, playable solo by recording both roles;
- soundscape or gesture sequence tied to textual details.

### Transform and create

- change one constraint and predict consequences;
- retell from a minor character/object/place, marking what the new narrator cannot know;
- create a missing artifact: notice, inventory, diagram, newspaper, instruction card, exhibit label;
- design an alternative cover only after identifying the book's central tension.

### Connect and compare

- same question, two books;
- text-to-place, text-to-life, or text-to-world connection with a stated reason;
- then/now comparison separating historical description from present judgment;
- motif bridge across books.

### Revisit and reflect

- three-word retrieval after closing the book;
- “I changed my mind because…” card;
- delayed character/idea reunion one week later;
- strategy card: what helped when confused or bored.

## Genre-specific patterns

### Myth and folklore

Build an origin/problem/power/consequence map; compare variants; create a rule for divine power; map natural phenomena; hold a solo audio “myth council.” Avoid treating one retelling as the only authentic version.

### Poetry

Use an image hunt, sound map, line-break experiment, emotion gradient, place map, performance choice, or two-translation comparison. Do not require memorization before meaning and pleasure.

### Continuous fiction

Track goals, obstacles, decisions, consequences, relationships, and unresolved questions. Use episode-based checkpoints, not daily page quotas. Protect suspense by avoiding premature summaries.

### Short stories and character sketches

Create a character evidence file, rumor-versus-proof table, entrance/exit study, one-minute storyteller performance, or comparison of two distinctive characters.

### Informational and science books

Use question trails, claim/evidence cards, observation missions, concept models, diagrams from memory, misconception updates, or a safe small test. Do not invent experiments that require unsupervised heat, chemicals, electricity, tools, heights, roads, or unknown plants/animals.

### Biography and history

Use a decision timeline, context map, source-perspective comparison, constraint inventory, or “what was knowable then?” card. Avoid presentism and hero-worship templates.

### Graphic novels and picture books

Use panel-direction tracing, silent-page narration, color/motif tracking, image-text contradiction, viewpoint cropping, or redraw one scene from a different distance.

## Variety rule

Across a multi-book plan, use at least four activity families. Within one book, avoid more than two activities with the same output medium. Always include at least one activity that leaves no written output. Apply the child-perceived play-rhythm audit above whenever the handbook has four or more core activities.

For a child-facing handbook, make at least half of core activities open-play patterns. Do not put a report, diagram, or written reflection after every reading. Treat writing and drawing as possible materials, not default proof of reading.
